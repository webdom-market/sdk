## What TON DNS Is In Practice

TON DNS should be explained as more than a naming gimmick. In practice it combines several layers:

- human-readable naming for TON wallets, apps, services, and sites
- NFT-style ownership of the domain asset
- public identity and branding layer
- market for collectible and investment-grade names

Useful framing for agents:

- protocol layer: readable names resolve to TON-native targets
- asset layer: the domain behaves like an owned on-chain item
- utility layer: it can be used for payments, sites, and service routing
- market layer: it trades, expires, renews, and accumulates status value

## Core Technical And Economic Facts

Important facts to keep in working memory:

- a domain is an on-chain asset rather than a centralized registrar entry
- TON DNS can resolve more than wallet addresses; it can also point to contracts, sites, and related TON resources
- subdomains can be delegated through smart-contract logic, so some names have platform potential beyond the root domain itself
- domains require renewal, so ownership is operational rather than purely passive
- exact renewal fees, auction floors, and venue rules are time-sensitive; verify current numbers before quoting them as facts

Practical implication:

- a strong domain is not just a string; it can be identity, infrastructure, and inventory at the same time
- expiry and renewal discipline matter when discussing portfolio quality or risk

## Primary Vs Secondary Market

Keep the two markets distinct:

- primary market: unregistered names enter through auction-style acquisition
- secondary market: already-owned names trade between holders and buyers

Do not answer primary-market questions with secondary-market logic alone. A low starting bid does not imply low end value, and a high secondary listing does not imply real liquidity.

When helpful, mention that:

- long names can enter at relatively low starting prices
- the secondary market has its own categories, watchlists, offers, analytics, and liquidity signals
- expiry and re-auction dynamics create opportunities and risks that do not exist in normal perpetual NFT narratives

## Main Platforms And Their Roles

Use these distinctions in answers:

- `dns.ton.org`: canonical entry point for TON DNS registration and basic management
- `webdom.market`: domain-specialized marketplace and operations layer with analytics, offers, protective portfolio tooling, and domain-focused workflows
- `Getgems`: broad NFT marketplace where domains are one asset class among others
- `Fragment` and `.t.me`: adjacent Telegram identity market, relevant as a neighboring pool of capital and attention, but not the same asset class as `.ton`

When a user compares venues, explain them by role instead of claiming they are interchangeable.

## What Market Structure Means For Answers

When answering about domain value or strategy:

- separate protocol utility from collectible value
- separate user utility from near-term liquidity
- distinguish official protocol facts from market heuristics and community narratives

Useful market observations:

- renewal pressure creates expiry risk and gives organized buyers an edge
- brandable names, dictionary words, short names, and identity-grade names usually have the clearest buyer stories
- some liquidity lives in narrower collector segments such as numeric or IDN names; do not treat those segments like broad end-user brands
- `.ton` and `.t.me` compete for attention, but they solve different identity problems
